## 🎯 Learning Objectives

*By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:*

- Clearly define **bid**, **ask**, and **spread**
- Explain how brokers get paid on every trade
- Calculate the spread cost in pips and in money for a given trade size
- Understand why spread and market conditions matter for their strategy

---

## 📖 LESSON CONTENT

### SECTION 1 — Two Prices for Every Pair

When you first open a Forex quote window, you see one pair and two numbers.

```
EUR/USD   1.0848   1.0851
            BID     ASK
```

There are always **two prices**:

- **Bid** — what the market is willing to **buy** the base currency for  
- **Ask** — what the market is willing to **sell** the base currency for

You don’t choose which you interact with:

- When you **sell** EUR/USD, you hit the **bid**
- When you **buy** EUR/USD, you pay the **ask**

The gap between them is the **spread** — the hidden tollbooth you pass every time you enter a trade.

---

### SECTION 2 — What Exactly Is the Spread?

Zooming in:

```
EUR/USD    1.0848 / 1.0851
               ↑       ↑
              BID     ASK
```

- **BID (1.0848)** — if you sell 1 EUR, you receive 1.0848 USD  
- **ASK (1.0851)** — if you buy 1 EUR, you pay 1.0851 USD

The **spread** is:

```text
Spread = Ask − Bid = 1.0851 − 1.0848 = 0.0003
```

On most major pairs, 1 pip = 0.0001.  
So 0.0003 = **3 pips**.

That 3-pip gap is how the broker gets paid on “zero-commission” trading.  
You start every trade a few pips in the red the moment you enter.

### SECTION 3 — How the Spread Becomes Real Money

To see how the spread hits your account, you need:

1. Spread in **pips**  
2. **Pip value** for the pair you’re trading  
3. **Number of lots** you’re trading

For many USD-quoted majors at 1 standard lot (100,000 units), 1 pip ≈ **$10**.

- Spread = 3 pips  
- Pip value ≈ $10 per pip (1 lot)  
- Cost = 3 × $10 = **$30** per trade

At 0.1 lot (10,000 units), pip value ≈ $1:

- Spread cost ≈ **$3** per trade at 3 pips.

Spread is not an abstract concept — it is real money leaving your account at entry.

---

### SECTION 4 — Why Spreads Change (and When They Hurt You)

Spreads widen and tighten depending on:

- **Market liquidity** — more participants = tighter spreads  
- **Time of day** — session overlaps are usually tightest  
- **News events** — high-impact releases can blow spreads out  
- **Pair type** — majors vs minors vs exotics

Examples:

- EUR/USD during London–New York overlap: often 0.5–1.5 pips  
- GBP/JPY outside major sessions: 3–6+ pips  
- USD/TRY around political events: 20+ pips is not unusual

For scalpers targeting 5–10 pips, a 4-pip spread is lethal — almost the entire move goes to the broker.  
For swing trades targeting 100+ pips, a 2–3 pip spread is tolerable friction.

### SECTION 5 — Spread vs Commission

Most brokers monetise you using one of two models:

- **Spread-only** — slightly wider spreads, advertised as “no commission”  
- **Raw spread + commission** — near-interbank spreads plus a fixed fee per lot

Which is cheaper depends on:

- Average trade size  
- Number of trades per day/week  
- Strategy type (scalping vs swing)

The important takeaway: you must **know your broker’s model** and factor total cost into your edge.

---

### SECTION 6 — A Simple Cost Example

You trade:

- **EUR/USD**
- **0.5 lot** position size (50,000 units)
- Broker spread: **1.5 pips**

Pip value at 0.5 lots ≈ $5:

- Spread cost = 1.5 × $5 ≈ **$7.50** per trade

If the trade wins 30 pips ($150) or loses 15 pips ($75), that $7.50 is acceptable — but it is always there.

Your job is to design a strategy where the **average reward vs risk vs cost** still leaves you with a positive expectancy.

---

## ✅ KEY TAKEAWAYS

- Every quote has **two prices**: **bid** and **ask**  
- The **spread** is ask − bid, measured in **pips**  
- Spread is how your broker gets paid on commission-free trading  
- Spread cost scales with **size**, **pair choice**, and **session**  
- You start every trade slightly negative — your strategy must overcome this friction
