## 📖 LESSON CONTENT

---

### SECTION 1 — The Simple Truth About Money

You've dealt with Forex before — even if you didn't know it.

If you've ever travelled abroad and exchanged your home currency for another country's currency at an airport kiosk, you participated in the foreign exchange market. You handed over one currency and received another at a specific rate. That rate — the price of one currency expressed in terms of another — is exactly what Forex traders trade.

But here's the key insight most people miss:

**That rate is not fixed. It changes every single second.**

The euro you bought for $1.05 today might cost $1.08 next week — or $1.02. Those movements are not random. They are driven by real forces — economic data, interest rates, geopolitical events, and the collective buying and selling decisions of millions of participants worldwide.

Forex traders study those forces. They try to anticipate which direction a currency's value will move — and profit from being right.

---

### SECTION 2 — What Exactly is the Forex Market?

**Forex** stands for **Foreign Exchange**. It is the global marketplace where currencies are bought and sold.

Unlike the stock market — which has a central exchange like the New York Stock Exchange — Forex has no physical location. There is no building, no central server, no single exchange. It is a fully **decentralised, over-the-counter (OTC) market**, meaning trades happen directly between participants across a global electronic network of banks, brokers, and institutions.

This has three major consequences:

**1. It never closes (almost).** The Forex market is open 24 hours a day, 5 days a week — from Sunday 5pm EST when Sydney opens, to Friday 5pm EST when New York closes. Trading flows around the globe as each major financial centre wakes up.

**2. It is enormous.** The Forex market trades approximately **$7.5 trillion every single day**. To put that in perspective — the New York Stock Exchange trades roughly $25 billion per day. Forex is roughly 300 times larger. It is the largest, most liquid financial market on Earth.

**3. No single entity controls it.** Because it is decentralised, no single bank, government, or institution can permanently control prices. Central banks can and do intervene, but sustained manipulation is nearly impossible at this scale.

### SECTION 3 — Why Do Currency Values Change?

Think about it this way. Imagine two countries: Country A and Country B.

Country A announces that its economy grew 4% last quarter — stronger than expected. Businesses are hiring, inflation is controlled, and the central bank just raised interest rates. Investors around the world now want to hold Country A's currency because it earns more interest and reflects a strong economy.

Demand for Country A's currency increases. Price goes up.

Meanwhile, Country B is dealing with rising inflation, a weak jobs market, and political instability. Nobody particularly wants to hold its currency. Demand falls. Price drops.

Every currency in the world is constantly being evaluated by this same logic — millions of times per day — by banks, hedge funds, corporations, governments, and retail traders. The collective result of all those evaluations is the live exchange rate you see on your screen, updating in real time.

The main forces that drive currency values:

| Driver | How it Affects Currency |
|---|---|
| **Interest Rates** | Higher rates attract foreign capital → currency strengthens |
| **Economic Growth (GDP)** | Strong growth signals → currency demand rises |
| **Inflation** | High inflation erodes purchasing power → currency weakens |
| **Political Stability** | Instability creates uncertainty → investors exit the currency |
| **Trade Balance** | A country that exports more than it imports sees currency demand rise |
| **Market Sentiment** | Risk-on environments boost commodity currencies; risk-off boosts safe havens (JPY, CHF) |

You don't need to master all of these right now. They will each get their own lesson in the Advanced module. For now, the key understanding is this: **currency values are not arbitrary — they reflect the perceived economic health and attractiveness of a country relative to another.**

---

### SECTION 4 — How Do Traders Actually Make Money?

Here is the core mechanic, stripped down to its simplest form.

Every Forex trade involves **two simultaneous actions**: you buy one currency and simultaneously sell another. This is why currencies are always quoted in pairs — EUR/USD, GBP/JPY, USD/CHF. You are always expressing the value of one currency relative to another.

If you believe the Euro will strengthen against the US Dollar:
- You **buy EUR/USD** (you buy Euros, you sell Dollars)
- If EUR/USD rises from 1.0500 to 1.0600, you profit from that 100-pip move
- You then close the trade by doing the reverse — selling Euros, buying Dollars back

If you believe the Euro will weaken:
- You **sell EUR/USD** (you sell Euros, you buy Dollars)
- If EUR/USD falls from 1.0500 to 1.0400, you profit from the 100-pip decline

The profit or loss is determined by:
1. **How far price moved** in your favour (or against you)
2. **How large your position was** (covered in detail in Unit 5)

That's the entire model. Buy low, sell high — or sell high, buy low. The complexity comes not from the mechanic, but from consistently predicting which direction price will move, and managing the risk when you're wrong.

### SECTION 5 — Who Can Trade Forex?

Two decades ago, Forex was almost exclusively institutional. Individual traders had no access. Today, online brokers have democratised the market. Anyone with a smartphone, an internet connection, and a few hundred dollars can open a Forex trading account and participate in the same market that banks and hedge funds trade.

This accessibility is both Forex's greatest opportunity and its greatest trap.

The barrier to entry is almost zero. But the barrier to **consistent profitability** is extremely high. Estimates vary, but research consistently suggests that between **70–80% of retail Forex traders lose money**. The reasons are almost never a lack of intelligence — they are almost always a lack of proper education, structured methodology, and emotional discipline.

That is exactly what this course addresses. Every module is designed not just to teach you what the tools are, but how professionals actually use them — and more importantly, how to build the mental framework that separates the minority who succeed from the majority who don't.

## 🎯 Learning Objectives
*By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:*
- Identify every major participant in the Forex market
- Understand each participant's motivation for trading
- Explain how the hierarchy of players affects price
- Recognise why understanding "who is on the other side" is a genuine trading edge

---

## 📖 LESSON CONTENT

---

### SECTION 1 — You Are Never Trading Alone

Every time you click "buy" or "sell" on your trading platform, someone is on the other side of that trade.

Not a computer. Not a random number generator. A person — or more likely, an institution — with their own analysis, their own agenda, and their own money at stake. They believe the opposite of what you believe. One of you is right. One of you is wrong.

This is the reality of trading that most beginners completely ignore. They stare at charts as if price is just a line moving randomly on a screen. But every single movement in that line represents a real transaction — a real agreement between a buyer and a seller on what a currency is worth at that exact moment.

Understanding **who those participants are** — their motivations, their size, their information advantage — is not just interesting background knowledge. It is a direct edge. When you know that price just swept a level where retail stop losses cluster, and you know that large institutions routinely engineer those sweeps to fill their own orders cheaply, you stop being the prey. You start reading the footprints they leave behind.

That knowledge starts here.

---

### SECTION 2 — The Forex Market Hierarchy

The Forex market is not a flat playing field. It is a hierarchy — a pyramid — where different participants operate at different levels of access, information, and influence.

From the top down:

---

#### 🏦 TIER 1 — Central Banks
*Examples: US Federal Reserve, European Central Bank, Bank of Japan, Bank of England*

Central banks are the most powerful participants in the Forex market — and they are not trading for profit. Their mandate is **monetary policy**: controlling inflation, managing employment, and maintaining economic stability for their country or region.

They influence currency values in two primary ways:

**Interest rate decisions** — When a central bank raises rates, its currency typically strengthens as global capital flows toward higher yields. When it cuts rates, the currency typically weakens. These decisions move markets violently — sometimes hundreds of pips in seconds.

**Direct intervention** — In extreme cases, central banks buy or sell their own currency in the open market to influence its price. Japan's Bank of Japan is famous for this — intervening directly when the Yen weakens too aggressively. These interventions can reverse weeks of price action in minutes.

Central banks don't trade every day. But when they act — or even when they *speak* — the entire market responds. Learning to track central bank policy is a cornerstone of the Advanced module.

**Key insight:** Central banks set the macro backdrop that every other participant operates within. Ignoring them as a retail trader is like sailing without checking the weather.

---

#### 🏛️ TIER 2 — Commercial & Investment Banks
*Examples: JPMorgan Chase, Deutsche Bank, Citigroup, Barclays, UBS, HSBC*

Commercial and investment banks collectively account for the largest share of daily Forex volume — estimated at over **40% of all transactions**. They operate what is called the **interbank market** — a network where the world's largest banks trade directly with each other, often in blocks of $100 million or more.

Banks trade Forex for several reasons:

- **Client facilitation** — When a multinational corporation needs to convert $500 million from euros to dollars to pay overseas suppliers, they call their bank. The bank executes that transaction in the Forex market.
- **Proprietary trading** — Banks run their own trading desks that speculate on currency movements for profit using the bank's own capital.
- **Market making** — Banks quote bid and ask prices to clients and other banks, profiting from the spread on millions of transactions.

The dealing desks at major banks have access to something retail traders do not: **order flow information**. They can see where large client orders are sitting — where clusters of stop losses exist, where large buy orders are waiting. This is an enormous informational advantage, and understanding it conceptually will sharpen how you read price action.

---

#### 💼 TIER 3 — Hedge Funds & Institutional Traders
*Examples: Bridgewater Associates, Renaissance Technologies, Millennium Management, Citadel*

Hedge funds are the most aggressive speculative participants in the Forex market. Unlike banks — which have client facilitation and market-making roles — hedge funds exist **purely to generate returns**. They take large directional positions based on macroeconomic research, quantitative models, or technical analysis.

Some of the most famous trades in financial history were Forex trades by hedge funds. George Soros's Quantum Fund famously shorted the British Pound in 1992, making over $1 billion in a single day when the UK was forced to exit the European Exchange Rate Mechanism — an event still known as **Black Wednesday**.

Hedge funds move markets. When a large macro fund decides the US Dollar is overvalued and begins building a short position over several weeks, that accumulation leaves footprints in price action — footprints that trained traders learn to identify.

Types of hedge fund strategies relevant to Forex:
- **Global Macro** — Trade currencies based on macroeconomic themes (interest rate differentials, growth outlooks)
- **Quantitative / Systematic** — Algorithm-driven strategies that react to data, price patterns, or statistical signals
- **Carry Trade** — Borrow in low-interest currencies (JPY, CHF), invest in high-interest currencies (AUD, NZD, emerging markets)

---

#### 🏢 TIER 4 — Corporations & Multinationals
*Examples: Apple, Toyota, Airbus, Shell, Samsung*

Large corporations are not trading Forex for speculation. They trade it out of **necessity**. A company like Toyota sells cars in the United States and receives US Dollars as revenue — but its costs are in Japanese Yen. To pay its Japanese employees and suppliers, it must convert those dollars to yen regularly.

These are called **commercial flows** or **hedging flows**, and they represent a significant and predictable portion of daily Forex volume.

Corporate flows matter to traders because:
- They create **recurring demand** for certain currency pairs at predictable times (end of month, financial year-end)
- Large corporations use **forward contracts and options** to hedge future currency exposure, which creates measurable positioning data
- At month-end and quarter-end, large-scale corporate currency conversion can cause notable price distortions — especially in the London fix at 4pm GMT

---

#### 📊 TIER 5 — Retail Traders
*That's you — and roughly 10–15 million other individuals worldwide*

Retail traders are private individuals trading through online brokers — platforms like MetaTrader 4/5, cTrader, or proprietary broker platforms. They access the market not directly, but through a broker who aggregates their orders and routes them to liquidity providers.

Retail traders collectively represent a small fraction of total Forex volume — less than 5% by most estimates. Yet the retail trading industry has exploded over the past two decades, driven by the accessibility of online platforms, social media, and the appeal of financial independence.

The uncomfortable truth about retail traders:

- They are the **least informed** participants in the market
- They have the **smallest positions**, meaning they have zero price impact
- They are **heavily marketed to** by brokers whose revenue model sometimes benefits when traders lose
- They make **predictable, emotionally-driven decisions** — clustering stop losses at obvious levels, buying breakouts that immediately reverse, panic-selling into lows

This is not said to discourage you. It is said to orient you correctly from the start. The traders who succeed in retail Forex do so by **thinking and acting differently from the retail majority** — using institutional logic, systematic risk management, and emotional discipline. That is the entire premise of this course.

### SECTION 3 — The Broker: Your Gateway (and Their Business Model)

There is a sixth participant that every retail trader interacts with but rarely thinks critically about: **the broker**.

Your broker is not on the pyramid above because they are not a market participant in the trading sense. They are an **intermediary** — they give you access to the market in exchange for a fee (the spread, or a commission per trade).

There are two fundamental types of Forex brokers, and the difference matters enormously:

**ECN / STP Brokers (Electronic Communication Network / Straight Through Processing)**
These brokers route your orders directly to the interbank market or a pool of liquidity providers. They make money on a small commission per trade. Their interests are largely aligned with yours — they want you to trade frequently and stay profitable long enough to keep trading.

**Market Maker Brokers (Dealing Desk)**
These brokers take the other side of your trade themselves. When you buy, they sell to you from their own book. When you lose, they profit directly. This creates a structural conflict of interest. Market makers are not necessarily predatory — most regulate themselves — but the incentive structure is important to understand.

**Practical guidance:**
- For beginner accounts, regulated market makers are generally safe and offer tighter spreads on small sizes
- As your account grows and you trade more actively, ECN/STP brokers with raw spreads + commission become more cost-effective
- Always verify regulation — FCA (UK), ASIC (Australia), CySEC (EU), NFA/CFTC (US) are the primary reputable regulators

### SECTION 4 — Why Knowing the Players Gives You an Edge

Here is where this lesson becomes directly actionable.

Most retail traders look at a chart and see candlesticks. Trained traders look at the same chart and ask: *who is buying here, who is selling, and why?*

Consider this scenario:

Price on EUR/USD has been rising steadily. It reaches a round number — 1.1000 — a level where thousands of retail traders have placed their stop losses (just above a round number is one of the most common retail stop placements in the world). Price pushes just above 1.1000, triggers all those stops, and then immediately reverses back down sharply.

Was that random? No.

A large institution — a bank or hedge fund — wanted to sell EUR/USD at 1.1000. But they needed to sell a very large position. If they simply placed a massive sell order, the market would move against them as liquidity dried up. Instead, they allowed — or engineered — price to push above 1.1000, triggering retail stop losses (which are buy orders, adding liquidity to the market), and used that liquidity to fill their massive sell order. Then price reversed.

This is called a **stop hunt** or **liquidity sweep**. It is not a conspiracy theory — it is a mechanical reality of how large orders get filled in a market with finite liquidity. You will learn to identify and trade these setups in depth in the Intermediate module.

For now, the lesson is this: **every candle on your chart is the result of a decision by a real participant with a real agenda.** The more clearly you understand those participants, the more intelligently you can read price.

### SECTION 5 — A Mental Model to Carry Forward

Think of the Forex market as an **ocean**.

Central banks control the tides — slow, powerful, inevitable forces that set the overall direction of the water. Commercial banks and hedge funds are the large ships — they create significant waves and wakes, and smaller vessels must navigate around them. Corporations are cargo ships moving on fixed routes — predictable, scheduled, large. Retail traders are surfers — we don't control the ocean, we don't create the waves. But with the right knowledge and skill, we can read the water better than anyone, position ourselves at the right moment, and ride movements that the larger players have set in motion.

The surfer who wipes out repeatedly is the one fighting the ocean. The surfer who succeeds is the one who understands it.

---

## ✅ KEY TAKEAWAYS

- The Forex market has a **clear hierarchy** — Central Banks → Commercial Banks → Hedge Funds → Corporations → Retail Traders
- Each participant has a **different motivation**: policy, client service, speculation, hedging, or profit-seeking
- **Central banks** set the macro backdrop through interest rate policy and direct intervention
- **Commercial banks** dominate volume and have access to order flow information retail traders don't
- **Hedge funds** are the most aggressive speculators and move markets with large directional positions
- **Retail traders** are the least informed and most predictable — which is why most lose
- **Stop hunts** are a real, mechanical phenomenon that exploits predictable retail behaviour
- Understanding the players transforms how you read price — from a random line to a record of decisions

## 📖 LESSON CONTENT

---

### SECTION 1 — The Language of Forex

Every market has its own language. Stock traders talk about tickers — AAPL, TSLA, MSFT. Bond traders talk about yields and duration. Forex traders talk in **pairs**.

A currency pair is the fundamental unit of Forex trading. It is not optional syntax or jargon — it is the core expression of what you are doing every time you place a trade. You are not buying euros. You are not selling dollars. You are simultaneously buying one currency **and** selling another, expressing a view on the **relationship between two economies.**

Until that distinction clicks, Forex will feel unnecessarily confusing. Once it clicks, everything else in this course will make more sense.

Let's make it click right now.

---

### SECTION 2 — Anatomy of a Currency Pair

Every currency pair has the same structure — two currency codes separated by a slash:

```
EUR / USD
 ↑       ↑
Base   Quote
```

**The Base Currency** (left side) is the currency being measured. It is always equal to 1 unit.

**The Quote Currency** (right side) tells you how much of that currency it costs to buy one unit of the base.

So when you see:

```
EUR/USD = 1.0850
```

It means: **1 Euro costs 1.0850 US Dollars.**

That's it. The entire quote, decoded.

Now watch what happens when the price changes:

```
EUR/USD moves from 1.0850 → 1.0950
```

The Euro got more expensive relative to the Dollar. It now costs 1.0950 Dollars to buy 1 Euro instead of 1.0850. The Euro **strengthened**. The Dollar **weakened** relative to it.

```
EUR/USD moves from 1.0850 → 1.0750
```

The Euro got cheaper. It now costs fewer Dollars to buy 1 Euro. The Euro **weakened**. The Dollar **strengthened.**

This is the entire logic of currency pair movement — and it applies to every single pair in the market.

### SECTION 4 — The Three Categories of Pairs

Not all currency pairs are equal. The market classifies them into three categories based on liquidity, volume, and spread cost — and your pair selection as a trader directly affects your cost per trade, your execution quality, and your strategic options.

---

#### 📌 MAJOR PAIRS
*The most traded pairs in the world — all include the US Dollar*

| Pair | Nickname | Daily Volume Share |
|---|---|---|
| EUR/USD | "The Euro" | ~28% of all Forex volume |
| USD/JPY | "The Ninja" / "Gopher" | ~13% |
| GBP/USD | "Cable" | ~11% |
| USD/CHF | "The Swissie" | ~5% |
| USD/CAD | "The Loonie" | ~5% |
| AUD/USD | "The Aussie" | ~5% |
| NZD/USD | "The Kiwi" | ~4% |

**Characteristics of majors:**
- Tightest spreads (lowest cost per trade)
- Highest liquidity (easiest to enter and exit)
- Most analysis, news coverage, and data available
- Cleanest technical analysis — price respects levels well due to high participation

**For beginners and most intermediate traders, majors — especially EUR/USD — are where you should focus.** EUR/USD alone accounts for more daily volume than all stock markets combined.

---

*Pairs that do NOT include the US Dollar*

| Pair | Nickname | Description |
|---|---|---|

**Characteristics of minors:**
- Slightly wider spreads than majors
- Still liquid and tradeable — just less so than majors
- Offer different technical behaviours — GBP/JPY for example is famous for its large, fast moves
- Useful when you have a directional view on two non-USD currencies

**GBP/JPY deserves a special mention.** It is nicknamed "The Dragon" for a reason — it can move 200–300 pips in a single session. It offers enormous opportunity but punishes undisciplined traders severely. It is not recommended for beginners.

#### 📌 EXOTIC PAIRS
*One major currency paired with a currency from an emerging or smaller economy*

| Pair | Description |
|---|---|
| USD/TRY | US Dollar vs Turkish Lira |
| USD/ZAR | US Dollar vs South African Rand |
| EUR/PLN | Euro vs Polish Zloty |
| USD/MXN | US Dollar vs Mexican Peso |
| USD/SGD | US Dollar vs Singapore Dollar |
| GBP/HUF | British Pound vs Hungarian Forint |

**Characteristics of exotics:**
- Wide spreads — often 3–10x wider than EUR/USD
- Lower liquidity — gaps and slippage are common
- Heavily influenced by local political and economic instability
- Not recommended for retail traders without specific knowledge of the underlying economy

Exotic pairs are a specialist area. Some institutional traders and hedge funds focus on emerging market currencies deliberately — the inefficiencies and macro opportunities can be significant. But the risk profile is entirely different from trading majors, and the spread cost alone makes short-term trading extremely expensive.

### SECTION 5 — Reading a Live Quote in Full

When you open a trading platform, you will see something like this:

```
EUR/USD    1.0848 / 1.0851
              ↑         ↑
             BID       ASK
```

You learned about bid and ask in Lesson 1.1 — but now let's contextualise it within a pair.

**The BID (1.0848)** — the price at which the market (your broker) will buy EUR from you. This is the price you get when you SELL EUR/USD.

**The ASK (1.0851)** — the price at which the market will sell EUR to you. This is the price you pay when you BUY EUR/USD.

**The Spread = ASK − BID = 1.0851 − 1.0848 = 0.0003 = 3 pips**

Every time you open a trade, you start 3 pips in the red. That is the broker's fee. You need price to move at least 3 pips in your favour before you break even.

On EUR/USD with a 3-pip spread and a standard lot (100,000 units), that spread costs you approximately **$30 per trade**. On an exotic pair with a 30-pip spread, the same trade costs you **$300** before you've made a single dollar of profit.

This is why spread awareness is not a minor detail — it is a core component of your cost of doing business, and it directly affects which strategies are viable on which pairs.

---

### SECTION 6 — Why Pair Selection is a Strategic Decision

Choosing which pair to trade is not a casual decision. It should be deliberate, based on your strategy type, your trading session, and your risk tolerance.

**For trend-following strategies:** EUR/USD, GBP/USD, and USD/JPY are ideal. They trend cleanly and respect technical levels.

**For range / mean-reversion strategies:** EUR/CHF and AUD/NZD historically exhibit mean-reverting behaviour within defined ranges — though this characteristic changes over time.

**For volatility-seeking strategies:** GBP/JPY and GBP/USD during the London session offer the largest average daily ranges among liquid pairs.

**For session-based strategies:** AUD/JPY and AUD/USD are most active during the Asian session. EUR/USD and GBP/USD are most active during London. USD pairs are most active during New York.

**A practical rule for beginners:** Start with one pair. Ideally EUR/USD. Learn its personality — how it behaves during each session, how it reacts to US economic data, how it trends and ranges. Mastery of one pair deeply is worth far more than shallow familiarity with fifteen pairs. Professional traders often specialise in one or two pairs for their entire career.

# 📝 LESSON 1.4 — The Bid, Ask & Spread

**Unit 1 | Beginner Module | Free Tier | Est. time: 9 mins**

---

## 🎯 Learning Objectives

*By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:*
- Clearly define **bid**, **ask**, and **spread**
- Explain how brokers get paid on every trade (even when you “pay no commission”)
- Calculate the spread cost in **pips** and **money** for a given trade size
- Understand why tight spreads and market conditions matter for their strategy

---

## 📖 LESSON CONTENT

---

### SECTION 1 — Two Prices for Every Pair

When you first open a Forex quote window, it looks deceptively simple — you see one pair and two numbers.

Example:

```
EUR/USD   1.0848   1.0851
            BID     ASK
```

Most beginners glance at this and think, “Ok, EUR/USD is around 1.0850.”  
That’s not wrong — but it completely misses the mechanic that matters for your bottom line.

There are **always two prices**:

- The **bid** — what the market is willing to **buy** the base currency for
- The **ask** — what the market is willing to **sell** the base currency for

You don’t get to choose. You interact with whichever side matches your action:

- When you **sell** EUR/USD, you hit the **bid**
- When you **buy** EUR/USD, you pay the **ask**

That small gap between the two is the **spread** — and it is the hidden tollbooth you pass every time you enter a trade.

---

### SECTION 2 — What Exactly Is the Spread?

Let’s zoom in on the previous quote:

```
EUR/USD    1.0848 / 1.0851
               ↑       ↑
              BID     ASK
```

- **BID (1.0848)** — if you sell 1 EUR, you receive 1.0848 USD
- **ASK (1.0851)** — if you buy 1 EUR, you pay 1.0851 USD

The **spread** is simply:

\[
\text{Spread} = \text{ASK} - \text{BID} = 1.0851 - 1.0848 = 0.0003
\]

In Forex, that 0.0003 is expressed in **pips**:

- For most pairs, 1 pip = 0.0001
- So 0.0003 = **3 pips**

That 3-pip gap is how the broker gets paid on “zero-commission” trading.  
You start every trade **a few pips in the red** the moment you enter.

---

*"The Spread Visualiser"*

> **What the animation shows:**
> A horizontal price axis for EUR/USD with two vertical lines: the left line labelled “Bid 1.0848”, the right line labelled “Ask 1.0851”. The area between them is shaded in red and labelled “Spread: 3 pips”. A marker representing “Your trade” jumps first onto the ask line when you buy, showing an immediate small unrealised loss. > Every trade starts negative by the amount of the spread. Smaller spreads mean less distance to breakeven.

---

### SECTION 3 — How the Spread Becomes Real Money

Pips are useful for thinking in relative terms. But your broker does not collect “pips” — they collect **money**.

To see how the spread hits your account, you need three ingredients:

1. **Spread in pips**
2. **Pip value** for the pair you’re trading
3. **Number of lots** you’re trading

For major USD-quoted pairs (like EUR/USD) at 1 standard lot (100,000 units), one pip is typically **$10**.

So in our 3-pip spread example:

- Spread = 3 pips
- Pip value ≈ $10 per pip (for 1 standard lot)
- Cost = 3 × $10 = **$30**

You pay that **every time** you open a 1-lot trade in those conditions — whether you later win or lose.

For a mini lot (0.1 lot = 10,000 units), pip value ≈ $1:

- Spread cost ≈ **$3** per trade at 3 pips.

The key insight: **spreads scale with your position size**.  
The larger you trade, the more every pip of spread matters.

---

### SECTION 4 — Why Spreads Change (and When They Hurt You)

Spreads are not static. They widen and tighten depending on:

- **Market liquidity** — more participants = tighter spreads
- **Time of day** — session overlaps (London + New York) are usually tightest
- **News events** — during high-impact news, many brokers widen spreads dramatically
- **Pair type** — majors usually have the tightest spreads; exotics the widest

Examples:

- EUR/USD during London–New York overlap: 0.5–1.5 pips typical spread
- GBP/JPY outside major sessions: 3–6 pips or more
- USD/TRY around political events: 20+ pips spread is not unusual

For a scalper aiming for 5–10 pips profit, a 4-pip spread is lethal — almost the entire move goes to the broker. For a swing trader targeting 150 pips, a 2–3 pip spread is acceptable friction.

---

### SECTION 5 — Spread vs Commission (and Which Brokers Use Which)

Most Forex brokers use one of two pricing models:

- **Spread-only** — you pay via wider spreads, advertised as “no commission”
- **Raw spread + commission** — spreads close to interbank pricing, plus a fixed commission per lot

Neither model is inherently “better”; it depends on:

- Your average trade size
- Number of trades you take per day/week
- Whether you scalp or swing

For small accounts and low trade frequency, spread-only with tight majors is usually fine. As your account size and frequency grow, raw-spread + commission often becomes cheaper overall.

The important part is that you:

1. **Know which model your broker uses**
2. **Factor that cost into your strategy**

---

### SECTION 6 — Putting It All Together in an Example

Imagine you:

- Trade **EUR/USD**
- Use a **0.5 lot** position size (50,000 units)
- Your broker’s spread is **1.5 pips** during London session

For EUR/USD at 0.5 lots:

- Pip value ≈ $5 per pip
- Spread cost = 1.5 pips × $5 ≈ **$7.50** per trade

If your average winning trade makes 30 pips ($150) and your average losing trade loses 15 pips ($75), that $7.50 friction is acceptable — but it’s still there on every single trade.

You’ll build a proper position sizing and expectancy calculator later in the course. For now, the key idea is simple:

> **Spread is not an abstract concept. It is real money leaving your account the moment you click “buy” or “sell.”**

## ✅ KEY TAKEAWAYS

- Every quote has **two prices**: **bid** (sell into) and **ask** (buy at)
- The **spread** is the difference between bid and ask — expressed in **pips**
- Spread is how your broker gets paid on “commission-free” trading
- Spread cost scales with **position size** and **pair choice**
- Tight spreads on majors are more forgiving; wide spreads on exotics are expensive and risky
- You start every trade in a small loss equal to the spread — your strategy must overcome this friction

# 📝 LESSON 1.5 — Forex Market Sessions

**Unit 1 | Beginner Module | Free Tier | Est. time: 10 mins**

---

## 🎯 Learning Objectives

*By the end of this lesson, the student will be able to:*
- Name the four major Forex sessions and their approximate trading hours
- Explain what **session overlaps** are and why they matter
- Understand how volatility and liquidity change across the 24-hour cycle
- Identify which sessions best match their own schedule and chosen strategies

---

## 📖 LESSON CONTENT

---

### SECTION 1 — A 24-Hour Market (That Still Has a Rhythm)

One of Forex’s biggest attractions is that it trades 24 hours a day, 5 days a week. But “always open” does **not** mean “always worth trading.”

Price moves most when:

- Major financial centres are open
- Deep liquidity is available
- Institutions are actively transacting

To understand when that happens, you need to know the **sessions** — the periods when each major region’s markets are open and active.

The four core sessions are:

- **Sydney** (Oceania)
- **Tokyo** (Asia)
- **London** (Europe)
- **New York** (North America)

Together, they create a rolling wave of liquidity around the globe.

---

### SECTION 2 — The Four Major Sessions (in UTC)

To avoid time zone chaos, we describe sessions in **UTC**. Your local time will differ — and your trading platform will usually show its own server time — but the structure remains the same.

Approximate session times (in UTC):

- **Sydney**: 22:00 – 07:00  
- **Tokyo**: 00:00 – 09:00  
- **London**: 07:00 – 16:00  
- **New York**: 12:00 – 21:00  

The key thing to notice is the **overlaps**:

- London + New York overlap for **4 hours** (12:00 – 16:00 UTC)
- Asia overlaps briefly with Europe as London opens

These overlaps are when:

- Liquidity is deepest  
- Spreads are tightest  
- Intraday volatility is highest  

Which is exactly what short-term traders want.

---

### SECTION 3 — What Tends to Move During Each Session

Each session has its own personality:

- **Sydney / Early Asian**
  - Quietest part of the day
  - Good for planning and reviewing, not usually for aggressive entries

- **Tokyo / Asian Session**
  - More active in JPY and AUD pairs (e.g., USD/JPY, AUD/JPY, AUD/USD)
  - Often sets the early range for the day

- **London Session**
  - The single most important session for many traders
  - High volume, tight spreads, especially in EUR and GBP pairs
  - Many major economic releases for Europe and the UK

- **New York Session**
  - Strong in USD pairs
  - Overlaps with London early on — often the highest volume period of the entire day
  - US economic releases (NFP, CPI, FOMC) can cause violent moves

Understanding which pairs are most active in which session helps you:

- Avoid trading a dead market
- Choose the right pairs for your available hours

---

### SECTION 4 — Session Overlaps: Where Energy Concentrates

The **London–New York overlap** (roughly 12:00–16:00 UTC) is the market’s equivalent of rush hour:

- Both European and US institutions are active
- Order books are deep
- Intraday trends often begin or accelerate

For intraday strategies, this window is prime time:

- Breakouts have more follow-through
- Pullbacks often resolve decisively
- Spreads on majors hit their tightest point

By contrast, late New York into early Asia often produces:

- Reduced volume
- Messy, choppy moves
- Wider spreads — especially on cross and exotic pairs

>

---

### SECTION 5 — Matching Sessions to Your Life (and Strategy)

You are not a full-time bank trader sitting in London with a 24/7 desk. You have **life constraints** — a job, family, geography.

Rather than fighting that reality, you design your trading around it.

Examples:

- If you live in **Europe** and work a 9–5:
  - Early-morning pre-work window overlaps with the **London open**
  - You might focus on EUR/USD, GBP/USD, and EUR/GBP

- If you live in **Asia**:
  - After-dinner window overlaps with Tokyo session
  - AUD/JPY, AUD/USD, and USD/JPY may fit better

- If you live in **North America**:
  - Morning before work can catch the last part of London and start of New York
  - US lunch break often overlaps the mid-New York session

In later units, you’ll decide:

- Which **pairs** you specialise in
- Which **sessions** you commit to trading
- Which **days** and **times** you deliberately stand aside (e.g., major news spikes if your edge doesn’t require them)

For now, the goal is simple: start thinking of time windows as part of your edge — not an afterthought.

---

## ✅ KEY TAKEAWAYS

- Forex trades 24 hours a day, 5 days a week — but liquidity and volatility are **not** evenly distributed
- The four major sessions are **Sydney, Tokyo, London, and New York**
- **Session overlaps**, especially London–New York, concentrate volume and movement
- Different pairs are most active in different sessions (e.g., JPY/AUD in Asia, EUR/GBP in London, USD majors in New York)
- Your edge must respect your **available hours**; you choose sessions that align with both your strategy and your life
